# External Context: Plasma Metabolites and Gait Performance

This brief summarizes recent (2019–2026) literature on the association between circulating plasma metabolites and gait performance (speed, stability, and risk of decline) in older adults and clinical populations.

## 1. Key Metabolite Classes Associated with Gait

### Lipids and Sphingolipids (Most Frequent)
Lipid profiles, particularly phospholipids and sphingolipids, are the most consistently reported biomarkers for gait speed and decline.
- **Lysophosphatidylcholines (LPCs):** Low levels of LPC 18:2 are an independent predictor of decline in gait speed over time. Source: Gonzalez-Freire et al. (2019). https://doi.org/10.1093/gerona/gly100
- **Sphingomyelins (SM) and Ceramides:** Alterations in SM (e.g., SM 16:1, 18:0, 18:1) and ceramides are associated with "dual decline" (simultaneous decline in memory and gait). Source: Tian et al. (2023). https://doi.org/10.1007/s11357-023-00792-8
- **Triacylglycerols (TGs):** Modules containing triglycerides are differentially abundant in those with dual cognitive-motor decline. Source: Tian et al. (2023). https://doi.org/10.1007/s11357-023-00792-8

### Amino Acids and Derivatives
- **Homoarginine:** Decreased synthesis of homoarginine is associated with dual decline in memory and gait. Source: Tian et al. (2023). https://doi.org/10.1007/s11357-023-00792-8
- **Branched-Chain Amino Acids (BCAAs):** Pathways involving Valine, Leucine, and Isoleucine biosynthesis are enriched in gait-declining phenotypes. Source: Tian et al. (2023). https://doi.org/10.1007/s11357-023-00792-8
- **Other Amino Acids:** L-proline and L-cystine are significantly changed in Motoric Cognitive Risk (MCR) syndrome, which is characterized by slow gait. Source: Li et al. (2022). https://doi.org/10.3389/fnagi.2022.977191

### Organic Acids and Others
- **Acylcarnitines:** Associated with gait speed in specific clinical populations (e.g., hemodialysis). Source: Moorthi et al. (2024). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-58522-9
- **IDO Activity:** Increased Indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO) activity is linked to gait and memory decline. Source: Tian et al. (2023). https://doi.org/10.1007/s11357-023-00792-8

## 2. Biomarker Panels and Prediction
- **Composite Metabolic Scores:** In dialysis patients, a composite score based on 12 metabolites (including acylcarnitines and organic acids) achieved an AUC of 0.88 for predicting impaired gait speed, outperforming clinical parameters alone. Source: Moorthi et al. (2024). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-58522-9
- **MCR Subtyping:** Lasso and Ridge regression models using lipids (PC, SM, TG) and amino acids can subtype "slow gait" phenotypes (e.g., MCR-III) with high accuracy. Source: Li et al. (2022). https://doi.org/10.3389/fnagi.2022.977191

## 3. Effect Sizes and Correlations
- **Correlation Magnitude:** Reported correlations between individual metabolites and gait speed are generally small to moderate (e.g., $r$ range from $-0.15$ to $+0.10$), highlighting the need for multivariate panels. Source: Gonzalez-Freire et al. (2019). https://doi.org/10.1093/gerona/gly100
- **Predictive Power:** While single-metabolite correlations are low, integrated omics models (Proteomics + Metabolomics) demonstrate high diagnostic performance for sarcopenia (which directly impacts gait speed). Source: Xu et al. (2026). https://doi.org/10.1002/jcsm.70188

## HPP Scope Caveats
- **Population:** Most studies focus on "older adults" ($\ge 65$) or clinical cohorts (dialysis, sarcopenia). The HPP 10K focus on 40–70 may capture an earlier transition phase of gait decline.
- **Measurement:** Many studies use "slow gait" as a binary risk factor (e.g., $<0.8 \text{m/s}$) rather than continuous stability/cadence metrics.
- **Study Design:** Several key findings are longitudinal (rate of change), whereas HPP is primarily cross-sectional. Associations found in cross-sectional HPP data should be interpreted as "snapshots" of the risk profiles identified longitudinally in literature.
